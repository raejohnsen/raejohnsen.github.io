"Suspiria (1977): Like Ballet, But with More Witchcraft and Blood"
★★★★☆



Oh, Suspiria, you saucy little devil! If ballet was a demonic ritual, Dario Argento's 1977 masterpiece would be its official instructional video. Picture this: pirouettes, pliés, and pentagrams – oh my!


First things first, let's talk about that technicolor nightmare fuel! The film's visuals are so vibrant, it's like someone spiked my eyeballs with a rainbow cocktail at a haunted disco. The bold use of color is like a neon sign that screams, "Welcome to Hell, population: YOU!"

Speaking of legends, let's not forget about that Goblin score. If you ever wondered what it would be like if a prog-rock band got possessed by a demonic conductor, wonder no more! It's as if they played the soundtrack on instruments forged from the souls of the damned. Don't be surprised if you find yourself humming the eerie melody in your nightmares.

And the witches – oh, the witches! Forget about those boring, pointy-hat-wearing hags you see in children's books. These witches are more fashionable than the entire New York Fashion Week combined. They have a knack for making even the most gruesome rituals look oddly stylish. Who knew that witchcraft came with a side of haute couture?

Now, let's talk about the deaths. People in this film meet their end in the most creative ways possible. It's like a twisted game of "Guess How You'll Die Next!" One moment you're hanging out in your room, and the next, you're swimming in a room full of razor wire. It's all fun and games until someone loses an eye, or an arm, or their entire face.

Jessica Harper as our wide-eyed protagonist, Suzy Bannion, seems to have enrolled in the school of perpetual bad decisions. "Oh, look, a creepy dance academy in the middle of nowhere that's basically a house of horrors? Sign me up!" Her journey from clueless American ballet student to witchcraft detective is the stuff of horror legend.
In summary, "Suspiria" (1977) is a masterpiece of horror cinema that's as captivating as it is bonkers. It's like ballet on acid, with more witchcraft and blood than you can shake a broomstick at. If you're looking for a film that will leave you both terrified and strangely mesmerized, this is the one. Just be prepared to question your life choices if you ever decide to enroll in a dance academy in the Swiss Alps.




"Suspiria (2018): Dance Like Nobody's Possessing You"
★★★★☆


Well, well, well, here we have "Suspiria" (2018), Luca Guadagnino's bizarre ballet of the occult, and let me tell you, folks, it's a whole different kind of dance recital. Imagine a traditional ballet performance, but instead of tutus, they're wearing blood-soaked leotards. Now that's avant-garde!
Dakota Johnson stars as Susie Bannion, who decides to join a dance academy in Berlin. Little does she know, it's not your typical dance academy where they teach you pliés and pirouettes; it's more like a crash course in witchcraft 101. Johnson's performance is as enigmatic as the plot itself, leaving you wondering whether she's here to dance or perform an exorcism.

The film's pacing can be a bit of a slow burn, but hey, it's like a fine wine – you have to savor it. The slow unraveling of the mystery keeps you engaged, and by the time you reach the film's climax, you'll be wondering if you accidentally wandered into some kind of unholy fever dream.
Tilda Swinton, on the other hand, is absolutely in her element, playing not one but multiple roles, because why not? One minute she's Madame Blanc, the intimidating artistic director, and the next she's Dr. Klemperer, the elderly psychotherapist who seems to have stumbled into the weirdest workplace ever. It's like watching an actor's version of "Freaky Friday" without the swapping bodies part.

The atmosphere is so thick with tension you could cut it with a knife. And speaking of knives, there's a fair bit of slicing and dicing that happens here. If you have a weak stomach, you might want to bring a barf bag because the gore is cranked up to eleven. It's almost as if Guadagnino had a checklist of how many gruesome deaths he could fit into one film.

Now, let's talk about the dance sequences. Oh boy, they're something else. They're like interpretive dance meets black magic meets an episode of "America's Got Talent" that went horribly wrong. It's like the dancers decided to give "The Exorcist" a run for its money with all the contortions and convulsions.
In summary, "Suspiria" (2018) is an ambitious, surreal, and occasionally bewildering reimagining of the cult classic. It's not your typical horror film; it's more like a descent into madness set against the backdrop of a dance academy that could use some serious renovations. So, put on your dancing shoes and get ready to waltz into a world where witches and ballet go hand in hand – or should I say, foot in spell?
